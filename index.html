Polaris, the North Star, guided sailors long before modern navigation systems existed, helping them find their path, especially by determining latitude. Today, while the world has transformed through technology, artificial intelligence (AI), and unprecedented access to information, the challenge remains the same: knowing where to go. With Polaris, we aim to provide a clear framework to help navigate increasingly complex markets and define the path ahead.

As Seneca wrote: "No wind is favorable for the sailor who does not know to which port he is bound."

Navigation Brief

Favorable Winds

Risk assets remain resilient, despite a tougher macro backdrop

Equities continue to price in a soft-landing scenario, supported by earnings strength and AI-related capex.

Credit carry remains attractive, but selection matters more

Yields around the 5–8% range still offer income cushion, especially in short-duration IG and selective HY.

AI remains the dominant capital allocation theme

AI capex continues to support technology, semiconductors, cloud, infrastructure and power-related assets.

Labor market weakness is contained for now

Employment is not accelerating, but there is still no clear recession signal from labor data.

Headwinds

Inflation is no longer improving meaningfully

U.S. core PCE rose 3.2% y/y in March, keeping the Fed far from declaring victory.

Energy has become a macro risk again

The Iran-related oil shock is feeding directly into inflation expectations and central bank caution.

Central banks are on pause, not pivoting

The Fed held rates at 3.50%–3.75%, while explicitly noting elevated inflation partly linked to global energy prices.

Europe faces the weakest macro mix

Eurozone inflation rose to 3.0% in April while Q1 GDP growth slowed to only 0.1%, increasing stagflation concerns.

On the horizon

  • Core inflation trajectory
    Any stickiness around 3% limits the case for near-term rate cuts.
  • Energy prices
    A sustained oil shock would challenge the disinflation narrative.
  • Fed communication
    The tone matters more than the pause: cuts require clear inflation progress.
  • Credit spreads
    Early signal of whether macro resilience is starting to crack.
  • AI capex sustainability
    The market is rewarding AI investment, but valuations depend on execution and monetization.

Macro Winds

  • The Fed is on pause, not pivoting
    Policy remains restrictive, with a high bar for cuts while inflation stays elevated.
  • Inflation is stabilizing, but not converging to target
    Core PCE remains around 3.2%, keeping real rates restrictive and duration sensitive to inflation surprises.
  • Growth is slowing, but still resilient
    The U.S. economy continues to expand, avoiding a hard-landing scenario for now.
  • Energy is re-emerging as the key inflation risk
    The oil shock introduces upside inflation asymmetry and complicates the path for central banks.
  • Geopolitics is re-emerging as a macro risk
    Ongoing tensions could disrupt the disinflation path.
  • Markets remain calm… but increasingly fragile
    Equities price resilience, while rates, energy, and geopolitics create a less forgiving backdrop.

April reinforces a market regime where carry still matters and AI continues to lead. However, macro risk is no longer declining. The key risk is not recession today, but a scenario where persistent inflation limits central banks' ability to respond if growth weakens.

Regional Routes

Europe

  • Growth remains fragile, with Germany expected to grow below ~1% in 2026, reflecting weak industrial momentum and soft external demand.
  • Inflation risks are re-emerging, driven by higher energy prices amid geopolitical tensions, introducing upside pressure just as disinflation was losing momentum.
  • Stagflation risks are back on the radar, as rising energy costs coincide with weak growth and limited demand recovery.
  • The ECB kept rates unchanged, but shifted to a more cautious tone, signaling reduced confidence in the disinflation path.
  • Equities face increasing valuation pressure, as elevated rates and weak earnings momentum begin to challenge current multiples.

Diagnosis:

Europe remains the most vulnerable developed region, where weak growth and energy-driven inflation create a stagflationary mix. With limited policy flexibility and a more cautious ECB, the region is increasingly exposed to rate repricing and downside risks in equities.

Asia

  • Japan remains in focus, with the BoJ proceeding cautiously as policy normalization advances, gradually influencing global yields and FX dynamics as domestic rates move higher.
  • China growth is stabilizing around ~4–4.5%, but remains uneven and policy-dependent, with continued weakness in the property sector and soft domestic demand limiting a stronger recovery.
  • Technology and AI-related sectors continue to support equities, particularly in semiconductors and hardware, driven by sustained global capex in AI infrastructure.
  • Oil-importing economies remain exposed, with higher energy prices creating upside inflation risks and adding pressure to external balances.

Diagnosis:

Asia remains a region of divergence rather than a unified macro story. Japan is introducing gradual tightening into global markets, while China continues to rely on policy support to stabilize growth. In this environment, equity performance is likely to remain concentrated in AI-linked sectors, with broader markets exposed to energy shocks and uneven domestic demand.

Emerging Markets (EM)

  • Growth expectations are being revised lower, particularly across energy-importing economies, where higher oil and food prices are eroding real incomes and external balances.
  • A clear divergence is emerging within EM, with commodity exporters benefiting from stronger terms of trade, while importers face renewed inflation pressure and weaker currencies.
  • Hard-currency debt remains attractive (~7–9% yields), but is increasingly sensitive to U.S. rates staying higher for longer and to dollar strength, limiting the scope for spread compression.
  • Capital flows remain fragile, especially in countries reliant on external financing, where tighter global liquidity conditions are beginning to reprice risk.

Diagnosis:

Emerging Markets are shifting from a synchronized recovery to a more fragmented landscape. Commodity exporters remain relatively resilient, while oil-importing economies face a more challenging mix of inflation pressure, dollar strength, and tighter financial conditions. In this environment, EM performance is likely to be increasingly selective, with greater dispersion across countries and asset classes.
